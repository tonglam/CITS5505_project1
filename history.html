The History of Hyperlinks

  • Pioneering Hypertext and Hypermedia

    1965

    In 1965, Ted Nelson, a self-styled "systems humanist," published "Complex Information Processing: A File Structure for the Complex, the Changing, and the Indeterminate" at the ACM '65 Proceedings of the 1965 20th national conference, 84-100. This groundbreaking paper introduced the terms "hypertext" and "hypermedia" to describe features of computerized information systems. Nelson utilised the term "link" to represent logical connections, later associated with the term "hyperlink."

  • The Birth of Hyperlink

    1965 (possibly 1964)

    The term "hyperlink" finds its origin in 1965 (possibly 1964) during Project Xanadu , led by Ted Nelson and his assistant Calvin Curtin. Inspired by Vannevar Bush's essay "As We May Think," Nelson coined the term as part of his visionary work. In the essay, Bush described a microfilm-based machine (the Memex) in which one could link any two pages of information into a "trail" of related information, and then scroll back and forth among pages in a trail as if they were on a single microfilm reel.

  • Bridging Text Strings to a Global Computer Network

    from 1964 through 1980

    In a series of publications spanning from 1964 to 1980, Ted Nelson revolutionized Vannevar Bush's idea of automated cross-referencing. Nelson applied this concept to the computer context, focusing on specific text strings rather than entire pages. He extended the notion from a localized, desk-sized machine to envision a global computer network. Throughout these works, Nelson passionately advocated for the realization of such a network.

  • Engelbart's Implementation

    1966-1968

    A team led by Douglas Engelbart, with Jeff Rulifson as the chief programmer, independently pioneered the implementation of the hyperlink concept. Initially, they introduced hyperlinks for scrolling within a single document in 1966. Subsequently, in 1968, they extended this concept to connect between paragraphs in separate documents, utilizing the NLS.

  • Shneiderman's HyperTIES System

    1980s

    Ben Shneiderman and Dan Ostroff pioneered the highlighted link feature in the 1983 HyperTIES system. This innovation played a crucial role in producing the world's first electronic journal, featured in the July 1988 Communications of the ACM. Cited as a foundational concept by Tim Berners-Lee in his spring 1989 manifesto for the Web, it had a significant impact on the evolution of digital publications. In 1988, Shneiderman and Greg Kearsley used HyperTIES to publish "Hypertext Hands-On!", the world's first electronic book.

  • The Impact of HyperCard on Navigation

    1987

    Introduced in 1987 for the Apple Macintosh, the HyperCard database program revolutionized hyperlinking. It enabled seamless navigation between pages within a document, as well as linking to other documents and separate applications on the same computer.

  • Windows Help 3.0: Hyperlinks and Interactive Help Messages

    1990

    In 1990, Windows Help, introduced with Microsoft Windows 3.0, gained widespread use of hyperlinks for connecting different pages within a single help file. It also featured a distinctive type of hyperlink that, when clicked, triggered a popup help message. This popup typically provided definitions for terms introduced on the help page.

  • The Evolution of Internet Hyperlinks: From Gopher to HTML

    1991-1993

    The first widely adopted open protocol incorporating hyperlinks between any Internet sites was the Gopher protocol, established in 1991. However, its prominence diminished following the release of the Mosaic browser in 1993, which could navigate both Gopher and HTML links. HTML gained an edge due to its capacity to blend graphics, text, and hyperlinks, offering a more versatile experience compared to Gopher's menu-structured text and hyperlinks.
